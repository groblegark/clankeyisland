# Editorial Desk — Merged Notes, 2026-06-12

## State of the show

Act 1 exists, runs end to end, and is funny on first contact — which is more than most slices can say, and is also where the good news stops. The bolt economy can't count, the act's signature duel plays itself, the GDD and the game disagree about whose key this is and which act the hideout opens in, and the premise of the entire game lives on a skippable hotspot. The meta-gag has been told nine times; it was funny once. Seven BLOCKERs, nineteen NOTEs, seven NITs. The desk recommends nobody write Act 2 until the BLOCKERs are settled, because three of them are about what Act 2 even is.

## BLOCKERs

### B1. The bolt economy is non-fungible and teaches the wrong lesson
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `tavern.scc:gusket:UsedWith` (`if(objB == InventoryItems::bolt)`) vs `alley.scc:dumpster` / `docks.scc:drain`
- **Note:** Reachable fairness wall: a player who enters the tavern before whacking the sign hears "Something... bolt-shaped", then can legally acquire NINE bolts (dumpster magnet and drain are ungated) and a tenth via the riddle duel — and Gusket rejects all of them with the generic "He polishes it politely and hands it back", zero nudge that the shim must be a different bolt object. The state stays winnable on paper (the sign whack is always available), but nothing in the world points back to the sign, and every failed offer actively teaches "bolts don't work on Gusket" — a knowledge soft-lock that walls the player in practice. See also the gate-count contradiction (N-A1).
- **Suggestion:** Accept boltStash/tenthBolt at Gusket (deduct one, swap stash art/text to eight), or gate the drain/dumpster behind servoFixed so the stall order is unreachable.
- **Desks:** Puzzles

### B2. The riddle duel — the act's named centerpiece — is a zero-input cutscene
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` (riddleDone branch) vs `docs/GDD.md` Core Mechanics ("Dialog trees with insult-fencing-style riddle duel vs. Rivet")
- **Note:** The GDD's one named signature mechanic, the Monkey-Island-homage centerpiece, auto-fires on TalkTo while holding boltStash. Sprocket answers Rivet's riddle himself, invents the winning riddle himself, and collects — the player makes no choice, risks nothing, can't lose. It can fire before the player has even seen the gate blink NINE, so the act's best comic tension (exact-change fare, off by one) can be skipped without ever existing. A full dialog-tree system (`game/dialog.scc`) sits unused in the codebase for the one scene it was designed for. And it's not alone: the dart hustle resolves the same way (give token, watch Flange self-destruct), so both of the act's contests are spectator victories — the player's contribution to Act One's climax chain is "clicked Talk and waited." A reviewer will write the sentence "the insult-swordfighting homage plays itself."
- **Suggestion:** Minimum viable: a dialog tree where the player picks Sprocket's riddle from 3 options (two flops with gag responses, one winner planted by the notice board). Gate the duel on having seen the gate's NINE rejection.
- **Desks:** Puzzles, Story, Player

### B3. The GDD and the game disagree about Key #1, and the hideout is a check nobody is scheduled to cash
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `docs/GDD.md` Act 1 + character table ("The Rustlers... stole the first Key") vs `game/docks.scc:crate:Open` ("A brass wind-up key... One of three") and `tavern.scc:doorBack:Open` / `alley.scc:hideoutDoor` ("Act Three, buddy" — twice)
- **Note:** GDD: Key #1 is recovered from the Rustlers' hideout. Game: Key #1 comes out of a crane crate as unwatched freight, and the hideout is locked behind a gag explicitly promising it opens in Act Three — but GDD Act 3 is entirely Old Crank and the Dynamo finale; there is no hideout in it. The implementation has written a check (a whole Rustler location, twice promised on-screen) that no document schedules anyone to cash, and the GDD still claims a theft that never happened. Meanwhile the key is mechanically well-earned but dramatically uncontested — no antagonist touches it, even with the fire-escape spy literally pointed at the docks taking notes — and the eavesdropped knock-code, the act's one genuinely earned secret, opens nothing. The climax has effort but no opposition. One of these must be rewritten before Act 2 hardens around the wrong one.
- **Suggestion:** Reconcile via the spyglass plant: the Rustlers steal Key #1 at talent night (Sprocket put it on a marquee while "in the notes"), making the GDD's theft true, giving the hideout its promised Act 3 content, and punishing the showboating — pick this or formally cut the hideout from the GDD.
- **Desks:** Story, Puzzles

### B4. The game's premise is optional
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `game/docks.scc:entry` (arrival cutscene, lines 99–117) + `docks.scc:board:LookAt` (knowsAboutDynamo gate)
- **Note:** The arrival cutscene establishes only "Guess I live here now" — never the Dynamo, never the keys, never any goal. The inciting incident lives entirely on the notice board's LookAt, a skippable hotspot; graffiti and the midtown entry assume the knowledge rather than supply it. A player can complete all of Act 1 — fix Gusket, hustle Flange, oil Betty, take the key, win the riddle, ride uptown, register for talent night — without ever learning what the Dynamo is or that anything is wrong. knowsAboutDynamo gates nothing on the critical path. The logline is not in the game; it's in a corner of the game.
- **Suggestion:** Make the notice-board read (or an equivalent forced beat) a hard gate — e.g., Rivet's first free fact, or the funicular gate refusing fare talk until Sprocket knows why uptown matters.
- **Desks:** Story

### B5. Verb roulette: Give silently fails where UsedWith is the answer
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `docks.transcript.json`: Gusket (no Give entry) vs Flange:Give / box office:Give / Rivet:Give
- **Note:** The natural verb for handing an item to an NPC is Give, and the transcript proves Give is a distinct, handled verb: Flange, Rivet, and the box office all have explicit Give arrays. Gusket and Boom-Arm Betty have only UsedWith. A cold player who tries "Give bolt to Gusket" and gets a generic decline will conclude the ITEM is wrong, not the verb, and will never retry the same combination with Use. Right idea punished by verb roulette is the classic quit-point, and it sits on the act's second puzzle.
- **Suggestion:** Alias Give to UsedWith on Gusket and Betty (or add Give arrays mirroring the UsedWith lines).
- **Desks:** Player

### B6. Sprocket asserts knowledge with no on-screen source
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `game/docks.scc:crate:Open` ("One of three, if the stencil is honest") + `inventoryitems.scc:windupKey:LookAt` + `midtown.scc:dynamo:TalkTo` ("one key and a lead on two more")
- **Note:** The stencil, as quoted to the player, says only "PROPERTY OF THE ORDER OF THE WIND-UP KEY" — it never says three keys exist, yet Sprocket credits "the stencil" for the count, and the inventory text doubles down ("The legends say so. The legends are a stencil"). Worse, the dynamo TalkTo claims "a lead on two more" when the player has no lead on any key — the line is reachable before meeting the clerk at all. This is the GDD leaking through the protagonist's mouth, and it teaches players that Sprocket knows things the screen never showed — fatal for a game whose narrator is the only information channel.
- **Suggestion:** Put the "three keys" lore in a reachable mouth (Rivet's first free fact is sitting right there) and gate the "lead on two more" line on metClerk.
- **Desks:** Story

### B7. The we're-in-a-script gag is told nine times
- **Severity:** BLOCKER
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:gate:LookAt` ("Uptown. Act Two. The future. It has a coin slot.") plus `docks.scc:crate` ("Act One: complete. Probably."), `tavern.scc:doorBack` ("'Act Three, buddy.'"), `alley.scc:hideoutDoor` ("'This is the BACK back door. STILL Act Three, buddy.'"), `alley.scc:gate` ("Act Two. I can hear the neon from here." / "Act Two will still be uptown tomorrow."), `midtown.scc:cityHall` ("Noted. Filed. Act Two material."), `tavern.scc:doorBack` ("Everyone in this town has read the script except me."), `alley.scc:dumpster` ("One manual for a better game")
- **Note:** It's one joke, told at least nine times across a four-room slice. The first telling (a CHARACTER — the slot-eye — saying "Act Three, buddy") is genuinely funny; every subsequent instance is Sprocket winking at the camera, exactly the telegraphing NARRATION.md forbids, corroding the dry, reported-secondhand register the whole game depends on. By the time City Hall material gets tagged "Act Two material," the meta layer has stopped being a surprise and become wallpaper — a reviewer will count these.
- **Suggestion:** Keep act-awareness exclusively in the mouths of NPCs (the slot-eye owns it; the second "STILL Act Three" echo earns its repeat). Strip every instance where Sprocket himself names acts — gate, crate, City Hall — and let him be the one bot who hasn't read the script, which is funnier anyway.
- **Desks:** Comedy

## NOTEs

### Act-wide / cross-file

**N-A1. Five consecutive puzzles are the identical shape; zero combinations**
- **Ref:** whole chain: `tavern.scc:gusket/flange`, `docks.scc:crane/drain`, `midtown.scc:boxOffice`; `GDD.md` ("Inventory combination puzzles (oil can + everything, ideally)")
- **Note:** Use single inventory item on single hotspot, watch cutscene, receive next item — bolt→Gusket, token→Flange, oilcan→Betty, magnet→drain, key→boxOffice. Both "contests" resolve as cutscenes (see B2), so even the beats designed as games are this shape. Not one inventory-on-inventory combination in the act despite the GDD calling for them, no dialog puzzle, no environmental-state puzzle. By Midtown the player has learned the game has exactly one move.
- **Suggestion:** Break one link's shape: the magnet should require combining magnet + something (string from the junk heap?) — it currently comes pre-assembled, the act's most obvious missed combination.
- **Desks:** Puzzles

**N-A2. Every lock names its key verbatim — and Gusket's hint cosplays as a joke**
- **Ref:** `docks.scc:crane:LookAt` ("O... I... L") + `crane:Use` ("She needs oil before she'll move that crate") + the crate's own line; `docks.scc:drain:PickUp` ("something magnetic. On a string"); `tavern.scc:gusket:TalkTo` ("Something small. Something... bolt-shaped.")
- **Note:** Betty names her key three times (morse, Use line, crate). The drain recites the inventory item's literal name before the player has seen the dumpster. Signposting is so far past fair-play that no Act 1 puzzle requires an inference; the player executes a read checklist. Worst case is Gusket: the ellipsis-and-pause before "bolt-shaped" is the game elbowing the player in the ribs — the single broadest deadpan break in the script, because deadpan never begs. The player is holding exactly one item and it is a bolt; naming the answer converts a tiny deduction into a fetch instruction, and the gag dies in the service of tutorializing.
- **Suggestion:** Keep one hint per lock and make at least one oblique (Betty's morse alone is plenty — cut the Use line's restatement). End Gusket's speech at "A little shim in the ratchet would free it."; if playtests stall, a second TalkTo adds the nudge — escalating hints, not a punchline-shaped answer key on first contact.
- **Desks:** Puzzles, Comedy

**N-A3. The "[material] and [abstract noun]" smell zeugma appears 14+ times**
- **Ref:** Smell verbs across all rooms — `docks:neonSign` "Ozone and civic neglect.", `tavern:rustlersTable` "Salt corrosion and bluffing.", `tavern:flange` "Hot bearings and confidence.", `tavern:gusket` "Bartender oil and quiet patience.", `alley:rivet` "WD-40 and good information.", `alley:dumpster` "Banana oil and municipal despair.", `inventoryitems:boltStash` "Brine and compound interest.", `midtown:cityHall` "Fresh paint and old decisions.", `midtown:boxOffice` "Ticket stubs and stage fright.", `inventoryitems:windupKey` "Brass and consequence.", `midtown:leftys` "Degreaser and optimism, in that order.", `docks:tavernDoor` "Spilled 10W-40 and broken dreams.", plus the near-duplicate pair `tavern:doorBack` "Rust, secrets, and back-alley grease." vs `alley:hideoutDoor` "Rust and secrets. The house blend." — the formula caught literally repeating its own output.
- **Note:** Not every Smell line has the disease — "Rust. Vintage rust. Estate rust.", "Trouble, marinating.", "Neon doesn't smell. The regret underneath it does.", and "Faintly of riddles." already break the shape — but the template laps the charter's 3+ threshold four times over, and after the third one the player can write the next one themselves.
- **Suggestion:** Keep the five best zeugmas and rewrite the rest into shapes the script already proves it can do — the flat fact, the sensor that can't parse, the escalating list. And make doorBack/hideoutDoor different jokes.
- **Desks:** Comedy

**N-A4. Punchlines that grade themselves**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` riddle cutscene — "He pays the bolt without a word. The best riddles aren't funny." (kin: `alley.scc:graffiti` "The whole town, one wall.")
- **Note:** The riddle beat is the best-written moment in the slice — "He looks uptown, at the Dynamo District." lands the whole thing silently — and then "The best riddles aren't funny." steps in to award itself a medal. The graffiti tag is the same disease milder: the three-layer wall IS the joke, and "The whole town, one wall." is the author's margin note. Deadpan means trusting the gap.
- **Suggestion:** End on the image in both cases: "...He pays the bolt without a word." full stop, and let NO REFUNDS close the graffiti.
- **Desks:** Comedy

**N-A5. The deadline does not exist**
- **Ref:** GDD logline ("three days... before everyone grinds to a halt") vs the implemented scenes; `alley.scc:gate:LookAt` ("Act Two will still be uptown tomorrow. Probably. If the Dynamo holds.")
- **Note:** No in-game line mentions three days or any timeline; the board says the Dynamo is failing, never when. The GDD's one designed pressure mechanism — the hum detuning as the game progresses — is unimplemented (Midtown plays the Shuffle at full cheer; "flat by half a step" is asserted against no audible baseline). Scene 05's sleeping spotlight operator is the first real escalation beat — good, but it's texture, not a clock. And the post-fare gate line actively tells the player there's no urgency.
- **Suggestion:** Implement the detuning hum (`genmusic.py` already parameterizes the themes) and tie it to act flags — the cheapest honest clock the GDD already designed; cut or invert the "tomorrow, probably" line.
- **Desks:** Story

**N-A6. Nobody wants anything except Sprocket**
- **Ref:** `tavern.scc:gusket` / `tavern.scc:flange` / `alley.scc:rivet` / `midtown.scc:boxOffice`; GDD character table (Mayor Piston: "Denies the Dynamo is failing")
- **Note:** Five scenes in, every NPC is a vending machine with a precondition: Gusket dispenses a token for a bolt, Flange an oil can for a token, the clerk a sign-up for a key; nobody pursues a goal across a scene, returns, or opposes. The nominal antagonist, Mayor Piston, is a banner, a notice, and a door opened nine degrees. The one live spark: Rivet initiates the riddle duel to win the official city notice — an NPC want the game never explains or follows up. Comedy in this register runs on NPCs who want things badly; this cast waits.
- **Suggestion:** Pull the thread already planted — why does Rivet want the notice? Make that want recur into Act 2.
- **Desks:** Story

### tavern.scc

**N-T1. The double-flagged piano punishes exactly the experiment it solicits**
- **Ref:** `tavern.scc:piano` (no UsedWith; "It's missing three keys... That feels thematically significant") and `alley.scc:fireEscape` (no UsedWith; "The ladder's rusted shut")
- **Note:** The piano is flagged "thematically significant" TWICE — the strongest "this is a puzzle" signal in the act — and the math invites a specific wrong move: piano missing exactly three keys, wind-up key "one of three." Players WILL use the wind-up key on the piano and get the generic "Those two things politely decline each other", which reads as "wrong forever" rather than "not yet" — a double-flagged red herring where players burn fifteen minutes and trust. Likewise the game teaches oil-fixes-stuck-metal via Betty (and stuck-mechanism-plus-small-item via Gusket's servo) then presents a "rusted shut" ladder with no oilcan response at all. In a use-everything genre this is where the comedy budget is supposed to live.
- **Suggestion:** Custom refusal lines for windupKey+piano ("Wrong kind of key. Probably. Filed under Probably.") and oilcan+fireEscape minimum; the piano one can plant the Act 2/3 keys payoff.

(Story desk notes the wink is at 2 of its 3-use budget; a third use converts it from plant to author underline.)

### alley.scc

**N-L1. The gate that "can only count" can't count the player's inventory**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:gate:Use` ("I feed it nine bolts. It counts them, blinks NINE at me"); nothing gates the alley/dumpster/drain behind the tavern chain
- **Note:** A thrifty player (the kind the bolt's own hoard-framing lines create) can go docks → alley → magnet → drain and arrive at the gate holding the original dock bolt PLUS nine from the stash — ten bolts in fiction. The gate, whose entire characterization is "Gates can count, it's all they do," counts nine, and the narration claims he fed it nine; Rivet appraises their pockets "Correctly" incorrectly. The text contradicts the player's visible inventory at the exact moment the puzzle is about counting. Progress survives, but both outcomes read as bugs from the chair, in the one puzzle whose theme is arithmetic.
- **Suggestion:** Branch the rejection when dockBolt is owned: the gate counts ten but rejects one as "non-circulating collectible" (keeps the joke, keeps the lock) — or just accept it; alternatively consume bolt #1 before the alley opens.
- **Desks:** Puzzles, Player

**N-L2. The act break is passable without the act's MacGuffin**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:gate:Use` (fareDone path; no windupKey check) → `midtown.scc:boxOffice:UsedWith`
- **Note:** Magnet, drain, duel, and fare have no dependency on the wind-up key or the tavern at all, so a player can pay ten bolts, ride the lit-up "Act Two" car uptown, learn the sign-up slot wants the key they don't have, and trudge back down to do the entire tavern/Betty chain — stalling at a clerk whose every line presumes a key. Not a dead end (ride down is free) but the staged act-break crescendo ("Act Two. I can hear the neon from here.") fires hollow, and the structure that the funicular scene exists to celebrate leaks.
- **Suggestion:** Have Rivet refuse to duel a nobody until Sprocket can show the key ("alley rules: stakes from somebodies only") — one line, closes the hole, feeds his character.
- **Desks:** Puzzles

**N-L3. The duel's prerequisites are narrated but never checked; Rivet is state-blind on his one defining trait**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` — metRivet branch ("He can see I don't have one"), riddle branch ("His bolt against my official city notice" / "What winds down... but never winds up?"); gated only on boltStash (`alley.scc:249`)
- **Note:** Rivet, whose entire characterization is omniscient appraisal ("appraises my pockets from four feet away. Correctly"), is wrong twice. (1) A player holding the dock bolt can meet him and be told "He can see I don't have one" — flatly false. (2) The wager is the official notice — poster pickup is fully optional (and its first PickUp attempt is a refusal, making skips likelier), so Sprocket narrates staking, and "winning", an item he never had: a continuity break on the act's climax puzzle. (3) The winning riddle is a Dynamo joke and knowsAboutDynamo is never checked, so Sprocket can deploy knowledge from a notice board the player never read. All reachable on the magnet-first route; all make the act's smartest beat play as a non sequitur.
- **Suggestion:** Branch the metRivet line on getObjectOwner(bolt); gate the duel on poster ownership + knowsAboutDynamo — or make the stake line conditional (name the notice only if held, else "his bolt against my dignity"). Until then Rivet says he only duels bots with something worth taking, which doubles as the poster's missing motivation.
- **Desks:** Puzzles, Story, Player

**N-L4. Rivet's advertised hint-selling is a transaction the game refuses**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` ("Second fact costs a bolt") vs `rivet:Give/UsedWith` ("names a price, and the price is an insult"); `GDD.md` (Rivet "sells hints for bolts")
- **Note:** Give bolt to Rivet hits the generic appraisal-insult line. This is an anti-signpost: dialog that explicitly teaches an interaction that doesn't exist. A player saving a bolt to buy the hint (a reasonable economic read of "bolts are currency") is being trained that the game's promises are decorative. The Act 1 "small economy" is otherwise two single-use tokens; nothing is ever priced or chosen.
- **Suggestion:** Make the purchase real: one bolt buys one hint line (dumpster/drain/tenth-bolt, whichever is next undone) — the cheapest way to make the currency feel like currency.

**N-L5. "It's all they do" is told three times, two of them back to back at the pinch point**
- **Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` "Gates can count — it's all they do." (first-meeting fact) / riddle-done fare-pending hint branch "'Ten bolts, exact change. It's all they do.'" / `inventoryitems.scc:tenthBolt:Use` "The gate does the math. It's all gates do."

### midtown.scc

**N-M1. Feeding the legendary key into a slot, reassured only after the risk**
- **Ref:** `midtown.scc:boxOffice` — Give ("I keep the key.") vs TalkTo
- **Note:** Signing up requires shoving the player's one legendary, one-of-three plot artifact into a machine. Adventure players are trained by decades of games to never feed plot items into slots, and the reassurance ("I keep the key. It's the act.") only arrives AFTER the player takes the risk. Loss-aversion will make many players exhaust every other option first; the clerk's "key-shaped, conceptually" is the lone nudge and it's also a joke, so it reads deniable.
- **Suggestion:** One clerk line up front: "You keep your props. House insurance won't cover a second Houdini."
- **Desks:** Player
