# Editorial Notes — 2026-06-12 (Scenes 05–07: Theater, Backstage, Oil Bar)

## State of the show

Seven scenes are on the floor and the slice has, mostly, stopped repeating itself: the Voltina reading and the Oil Bar are real new shapes, and the heist cashes the spyglass and the knock-code like a desk that plans its plants. But the act trades old diseases for new ones in the same beat. The talent-night centerpiece still plays itself — the winning option wears a sign — while the Oil Bar two doors down proves the team can plant a contest fairly, so we can A/B the author against himself. The GDD has drifted open again on the exact seam B3 closed, two heavily-planted payoffs (work order, piano) have nobody scheduled to cash them, and the power ledger you spent three lines installing gets quietly overspent the first time it's inconvenient. Comedy is sharp and over-served: the clipped deadpan stinger fires four times in one room. The build is good. It is also, in four named places, telling the player the answer.

## BLOCKERs

### B-01 — The talent act still plays itself
**Severity:** BLOCKER
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage:UsedWith` (windupKey branch, dialog options 327–334); emcee prompt "The manual mentions a number. In bold. In red."
**Desks:** Puzzle
The act is interactive now (the old B2 fix), but the choice is fake: the winning option is the only one whose own text announces it is the answer. Options are "Three turns. A dignified minimum.", "Five turns. The regulation maximum.", and "Eight turns. THE BOLD RED NUMBER." The player is told to pick "the bold red number" and one option is literally captioned that. Nothing in the game ever shows the number — grepped: no manual page, no "8", no red-number plant in docks/tavern/midtown/inventory or the dumpster's "manual in a language of pure grease." The player doesn't infer 8; they match a label to a prompt. This is B2's disease re-skinned as a dialog, and it's the more glaring because the very next scene's contest (Oil Bar) does it correctly — see N-07.
**Suggestion:** Plant the number diegetically and strip it from the option label. Put "8" on the wind-up key itself (LookAt: "a red 8 stamped under the bow — somebody's idea of a redline") or on the dumpster manual, then make the options bare ("Three." / "Five." / "Eight.") so the choice tests whether the player read the key, not whether they can match two strings.

### B-02 — The work order promises a showdown the design never schedules
**Severity:** BLOCKER
**Ref:** `inventoryitems.scc:workOrder:LookAt`+`Use` ("Paper this heavy wants a desk to land on… City Hall, nine degrees open"); `oilbar.scc:aide` expose ("Take it to him. Aides can't.") vs `docs/GDD.md` (no Mayor confrontation in Act 3); `midtown.scc:cityHall:UsedWith` (refusal only)
**Desks:** Story (structural), Player (signposting)
The work order is the most heavily-planted payoff-promise in the game and nothing is scheduled to cash it. Its Use line points at City Hall's nine-degree door; the aide explicitly commissions the errand; City Hall already has a poster-rejection beat with a door that closes to one degree "loaded." Every arrow points at a Mayor Piston confrontation — and the GDD's Act 3 is entirely Old Crank and the Dynamo finale, with no City Hall scene. The player walks out of the Oil Bar holding signed evidence, told to take it to the Mayor, into an act that has no Mayor in it. This is B3's exact disease one tier up, worse because the aide gives a verbal quest the design never honors. From the chair (Player), the held item's most authoritative hint sends the player to a hotspot that only rejects them — reading as "wrong target" rather than "not yet."
**Suggestion:** Either schedule the City Hall confrontation in GDD Act 3 (the loaded door + work order is the obvious lock-and-key) **and** gate the workOrder Use line / aide's "take it to him" on Act 3 state — or rewrite the lines so the payoff is internal to the Dynamo finale (it proves Old Crank was fired, earning his trust). Whichever path, make City Hall's refusal name the gap ("not without the other keys") so it reads as locked, not wrong.

### B-03 — The stage LookAt prints the same sentence twice
**Severity:** BLOCKER
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage:LookAt`, lines 304–306
**Desks:** Comedy (lead), Puzzle
The pre-show LookAt prints the exact same sentence twice, back to back, with only a `waitForMessage` between: "The stage. Tonight it gets a maintenance-bot and a very large key." / (beat) / "Tonight it gets a maintenance-bot and a very large key." No escalation, no clause, no variation. Every other deliberate echo in the script changes something on the second pass (the docks sign's POP. joke gains a clause; the notice's "nothing is wrong"; City Hall's banner). This repeats word-for-word, so it reads as a copy-paste left in the file — and it sits on the LookAt of the act's centerpiece object, where players land early and often. A reviewer hits this and assumes the build is broken.
**Suggestion:** Cut the duplicate, or land the second pass: "The stage. Tonight it gets a maintenance-bot and a very large key." / "The key is the act. The maintenance-bot is the optimism." Do **not** reuse "Everyone saw a triumph / I saw a hatch" — that already ships verbatim at `theater.scc:392`.

### B-04 — One state, two incompatible physical causes
**Severity:** BLOCKER
**Ref:** `theater.scc:spotBooth:LookAt` (line 112) vs `theater.scc:emcee:TalkTo` (line 259)
**Desks:** Embodiment
The spotlight operator's "low-power mode" is given two contradictory causes in one room. The booth's LookAt says brownout: "in low-power mode, eye dimmed to a polite ember… The brownouts reach everywhere." The emcee says boredom: "the spotlight operator went into low-power mode during the juggler. The juggler had that effect." One reading is the act's only real escalation (the dying Dynamo is now switching bots OFF mid-show — stakes). The other turns the same physiology into a nap a bad act induced, contradicting the power model: per `docs.scc:422` a bot's power is the hum keeping its mainspring wound, so a bot cannot elect to go low-power "because it was bored" — low-power is the hum failing *to* it. Pick one. If it's the brownout, the joke goes; if it's the joke, the brownout line is lying and the act's stakes evaporate.
**Suggestion:** Cut the emcee's "the juggler had that effect" clause; let both lines agree the brownout did it ("the brownout caught him mid-juggler"), preserving the escalation the booth LookAt already planted.

### Scene 05 — Theater (`theater.scc`)

**N-01 — Scene 05 contains no fair-play deduction**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Puzzle
**Ref:** `theater.scc:spotBooth:Use`/`TalkTo`/`Move` (spotAwake gate) + emcee/stage "no spot, no act" lines
The only puzzle gating the act is "wake the spotlight operator," and waking him is a single unconditioned click on the booth — no item, no inference, no prerequisite (Use/TalkTo/Move all just set spotAwake=1). The fair-play scaffolding is heavy and triple-stated (emcee: "no spot, no act"; stage: "the operator is asleep"; booth LookAt: "in low-power mode") for a lock whose key is "click the lock." Scene 05's interactive content is therefore really one thing — the wind-count menu — which per B-01 isn't a puzzle either. As implemented, Scene 05 has no fair-play deduction in it.
*Tension to resolve:* the Player desk audited this same gate as the slice's **best-signposted** new puzzle (three independent sources converging on the verb without naming it) and asked a future pass not to "simplify the redundancy away." The defense pass should reconcile: the redundancy is fair, but it gates a click, not a thought.
**Suggestion:** Make waking the operator cost something to think about (he's down from the brownout — hand-wind him? feed him from the oil can if it weren't spent?), or lean in and cut the triple signposting so the beat reads as the deliberate gag it is.

**N-02 — Self-winding is the universal backup, not a marvel**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Embodiment
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage` (line 349, "I wind myself. Eight full turns") + `midtown.scc:boxOffice` (line 397) vs `docks.scc:board` (line 424) + `backstage.scc:179`
The power ledger you just installed makes self-winding the universal backup. `docs.scc:424`: "Hand-winding is the backup. Crude, short-burst, off-warranty." `backstage.scc:179`, of Old Crank: "He was winding himself before the city needed winding." So every bot has a winding port and the means to crank it. A maintenance-bot publicly self-winding is the crude desperation move of a failing city — a great dark joke — but the theater plays it as a never-seen novelty the clerk and emcee marvel at. The marvel and the ledger can't both be true.
**Suggestion:** Lean into the seam: the emcee marvels not that he *can* self-wind but that he'll do it PROUD ("Nobody admits they're on backup. He's selling it") — which keeps both the marvel and the ledger.

**N-03 — The heist's biggest beat relapses on self-grading punchlines**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Comedy
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage` win cutscene — line 371 ("the most honest act… I don't correct him"), line 392 ("Everyone in this house saw a triumph. I saw a hatch.")
The ledger claims N-A4 (self-grading punchlines) was fixed; it relapsed in the new scene's biggest beat. "I don't correct him" is Sprocket awarding his own honesty a medal — the "the best riddles aren't funny" move the desk killed last pass, reworded. And "Everyone saw a triumph. I saw a hatch." is a strong, quiet image undercut by being the THIRD time the cutscene says the triumph was secretly a robbery (the key vanished at 382, the knuckles signed at 388).
**Suggestion:** End the heist on the knuckle-code at 388 ("They sign their work.") and the hat/spyglass recognition at 390; cut the "I don't correct him" self-grade and trim the "Everyone saw a triumph" gloss as the third restatement. For the landing, trust the image: "I land in the spotlight. On my feet." Full stop.

**N-04 — The narrator does the player's inference at the theft**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Comedy
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage` win, line 384 ("…Somebody else's magnet-on-a-string.")
The heist hangs the theft of Key #1 on the narrator spelling out that the Rustlers used a magnet-on-a-string — the player's own signature tool, named explicitly. The hat (390) and spyglass (390) already identify the Rustlers; calling out "magnet-on-a-string" is a third, redundant, on-the-nose identifier. Worse, it's a wink — Sprocket pointing at the parallel instead of letting the player notice it and feel it. Deadpan reports; it doesn't elbow.
**Suggestion:** Let the tackle stay unexplained: "A hook on a line whips up past the proscenium and is gone." The player who fished the drain with a magnet-on-a-string makes the connection unprompted, which is the whole pleasure.

**N-05 — Two doctor-voice gags in one room**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Comedy
**Ref:** `theater.scc` — line 417 ("'Safe,' says the emcee, like a diagnosis."), line 273 ("the way doctors say 'this won't hurt'")
Scene 05 introduces a new repeated shape: the medical/clinical simile. "like a diagnosis" and "the way doctors say 'this won't hurt'" are the same joke within ~150 lines, and they rhyme with the older Betty "Doctor's orders" bits. Two doctor-voice gags in one room blunts the first.
**Suggestion:** Keep "the way doctors say 'this won't hurt'" (better, and it motivates the stage-fright). Re-cut the emcee's "Safe" tag to a showbiz register: "'Safe,' says the emcee, the word landing like a coat-check ticket."

**N-06 — The proscenium-as-sparring-partner fires twice on one object**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Comedy
**Ref:** `theater.scc` — line 361 ("The proscenium and I meet several times. It gives as good as it gets.") + line 438 ("The proscenium and I have an agreement.")
The personified-architecture construction ("the X and I") fires twice on the same object in one room, and it's house style at large (the jar and I are square, the rope and I are past it, the stool and I), so the in-room doubling makes a franchise tic visible. **N-08 — The heist swaps a key for a key and nobody re-states the goal**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Story
**Ref:** `theater.scc:stage:UsedWith` (heist cutscene) → backstage entry
The heist is earned, but it inverts Sprocket's goal without restating it. Pre-heist Sprocket wants Key #1; the heist takes it back at the bow — and the very next thing the player does is walk backstage to BUY Key #2 from Voltina. **N-09 — The visible prize is dangled before the toll is introduced**

### Cross-scene (chain shape & economy)

**N-11 — Every set-piece contest is now the identical interaction, four times**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Puzzle, Story
**Ref:** `alley.scc:rivet:TalkTo` (riddle duel), `theater.scc:stage:UsedWith` (wind count), `oilbar.scc:sommelier:TalkTo` (two rounds), `backstage.scc` reading
The old monotony charge (N-A1, every scene one fetch) is genuinely fixed — the reading and the Oil Bar are real new shapes, credit due. But a new sameness replaces it: every contest is the same machine — open a 3-option dialog, two options flop with a gag and a free retry, one wins. Rivet's duel, the wind count, and both Oil Bar rounds are that machine four times. By the Oil Bar the player has learned the meta-rule "one of three is right, the others are jokes, guess freely, no cost" — which removes the tension contests are supposed to create. The riddle duel and the reading survive because their content does work (the reading answers cards with your carried route, Card III is a confession); the wind count is pure shape; the Oil Bar rounds are saved only by being well-planted.
**Suggestion:** Give at least one contest stakes or a non-menu input. The wind count is the candidate: make over-winding consume a beat or escalate the emcee's patience, or make the riddle duel cost the staked notice on a wrong answer (re-pickupable, but the loss is felt). One contest that isn't a consequence-free menu re-arms all four.

**N-12 — Seven scenes with no inventory-on-inventory combination on the critical path**
**Severity:** NOTE · **Desks:** Puzzle
**Ref:** `midtown.scc:boxOffice:UsedWith` (signedUp gates on metClerk) + the funicular→talent-night spine
The spine is still almost entirely single-item-on-single-hotspot (bolt→Gusket, token→Flange, oilcan→Betty, magnet→drain, key→boxOffice, key→stage, voucher→rope, poster/voucher/pass→reading table).
